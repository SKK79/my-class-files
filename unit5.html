🐒 The Monkeys Go Fasting

One day, a group of monkeys decided to go on a fastfast /fɑːst/ (n./v.): to go without eating food for a period of time, usually for religious reasons. The old monkey chiefchief /tʃiːf/ (n.): a leader or head of a group thought that they would be starvedstarved /stɑːvd/ (v.): suffered greatly from hunger; had very little or no food soon if no food was stored. So he called the younger monkeys and suggested, "Before we begin fasting, I think you should collect some food. We can take it immediately after our fasting is over." The monkeys noddednodded /ˈnɒdɪd/ (v.): moved the head up and down to show agreement or understanding their heads in agreement. The youngsters were sent in search of food. They returned with huge hands of deliciousdelicious /dɪˈlɪʃəs/ (adj.): very tasty; having a very pleasant flavour-looking bananas.


Looking at those bananas, the wife of the chief monkey counselledcounselled /ˈkaʊnsəld/ (v.): gave advice to someone, "Why don't you keep your share of bananas with you before we begin our fast. We won't get time distributing the bananas after we break our fast. You can imagine how hungry we all will be by then!"


The monkeys liked the idea and they collected their share of the bananas. "Why don't we peel one banana and keep it ready to eat?" said one of the youngsters. "Yes, let's do that," shouted a fat monkey in agreement. Just looking at the bananas was making him hungry.


"All right," said the monkey chief. "We shall peel the bananas but we should not eat them until the fasting is over." So the monkeys peeled their bananas and carefully kept them ready for eating in the evening.


"Can I keep the banana in my mouth? I promisepromise /ˈprɒmɪs/ (v.): to make a commitment to do something not to eat it till evening. Please!" a little monkey asked his father. "Why don't we all put a banana in our mouth? That way we can chew it immediately when we break the fast," said his father. "If we don't eat it, it won't harm our agreement," he added.


So, the monkeys put the bananas in their mouths. One by one they looked at each other uncomfortablyuncomfortably /ʌnˈkʌmftəbli/ (adv.): in a way that causes discomfort or unease as they began their fasting. Within no time at all, the bananas disappeared down their gulletsgullets /ˈɡʌlɪts/ (n.): the passages from the mouth to the stomach; throats. And that was the end of their fast!

(Adapted from Indian Folklores)

Why don't you + verb …   → Why don't you speak politely?

📐Grammar — Type 2 Conditional8 marks · Auto-scored
💡 Type 2 Conditional: Used for imaginary or unlikely situations in the present/future.
  • Structure: If + past simple, … would + infinitive
  • If I were a bird, I would fly high. (I am not a bird — imaginary!)
  • If she asked me, I would tell her the answer.
  • I would buy a house if I had enough money.

💻 History of Computers

The first computers were very big. They were the size of a room! They were so big that people didn't have them at home. Every computer could also only do simple math, like a calculatorcalculator /ˈkælkjuleɪtə/ (n.): a small device used for doing mathematical calculations. In the 1930s, Alan Turing had the idea for a computer you could programmeprogramme /ˈprəʊɡræm/ (v.): to write instructions for a computer to follow to do different things.


In 1958, Jack Kilby inventedinvented /ɪnˈventɪd/ (v.): created or designed something for the first time the microchipmicrochip /ˈmaɪkrəʊtʃɪp/ (n.): a tiny electronic component that can store lots of information. Microchips are tiny but can store lots of information. They helped make computers smaller. In the 1970s, computers were smaller and cheaper so people started to use them at home. In the 1980s, computer games were very popular. Lots of people bought computers just to play games.


In 1989, Tim Berners Lee invented the World Wide WebWorld Wide Web (WWW): a system of websites and web pages connected through the internet (WWW) which is a way to organise information on the internetinternet /ˈɪntənet/ (n.): the global system of connected computers that allows sharing of information. Now, people all over the world can look for and share information on websites.


Today, people can use smartphonessmartphones /ˈsmɑːtfəʊnz/ (n.): mobile phones that can connect to the internet and run applications to play games, email and go on the internet. In the past, a simple computer was the size of a room. Now, it can go in your pocket!

Gairigaun: Then and Now

MukeshMukesh /muːkɛʃ/ (n.): the name of an eleven-year-old boy living in Gairigaun village is an eleven year old boy. He lives in Gairigaun. He studies in class six. One day, his English teacher told that the village was different in the past than what it is now. He could not believe it. He decided to ask about it to his grandfather who knew many things about the past. As soon as he reached home, he went to his grandfather. His grandfather told Mukesh that many things have been changed in the village in his life time. His grandfather's story attractedattracted /əˈtræktɪd/ (v.): drew interest or attention; fascinated someone strongly him very much.

This is what his grandfather said: There had been a peacefulpeaceful /ˈpiːsfəl/ (adj.): calm and quiet; free from disturbance or violence travellers' rest in the middle of the village. People used to call it 'Bar Pipal ChautaroBar Pipal Chautaro /bɑːr pɪpəl tʃɔːtɑːroʊ/ (n.): a traditional resting place under bar and pipal trees found in Nepali villages'. Many travellers used to take rest at the Chautaro. Pipal was bigger than Bar, I think. I used to spend a lot at the Chautaro and meet many people. People used to come there for rest and recreationrecreation /ˌrekriˈeɪʃən/ (n.): activity done for enjoyment and relaxation during leisure time. We could hear melodiousmelodious /məˈloʊdiəs/ (adj.): sweet-sounding and pleasant to the ear; musical songs by the birds while sitting at the Chautaro. There were large, plain, smooth stones where people used to sit and talk about different matters about the village. There was a water tap nearby. Children used to play different games there. Sometimes, they used to spend the whole day playing there, even without eating any snacks in the afternoon. The school going children became very happy when they got some time to have rest at the Chautaro. While returning home late, they used to spend longer time at the Chautaro. That was a publicpublic /ˈpʌblɪk/ (adj.): belonging to the community; for use by everyone, not just one person propertyproperty /ˈprɒpəti/ (n.): belongings; something owned by a person or a community of the villagers.

But now, Mukesh finds the Chautaro no more there. Both Bar and Pipal have been cut downcut down /kʌt daʊn/ (phrasal v.): to fell or chop a tree so it falls to the ground and the beauty of the old place has gone away. A road has been constructedconstructed /kənˈstrʌktɪd/ (v.): built; put together using materials and planning by the Chautaro. People have lost the place to rest.

📌 Grammar Rules: Used to

✅ Affirmative: She used to have long hair, but now it's short.
❓ Question: Did they use to have a dog?
❌ Negative: It didn't use to snow a lot in winter.
📌 We use used to for habits or states in the past that no longer happen or exist now.
⚠️ Note: In questions and negatives, we use use to (not used to) after did/didn't.

Anne Frank: A Short Life

1929: Anne FrankAnne Frank /æn fræŋk/ (n.): a Jewish girl born in Germany (1929–1945) famous for her diary written while hiding from the Nazis was born in Frankfurt, Germany on 12 June. Her family was Jewish, and she is the second daughter.

1933: Adolf HitlerAdolf Hitler /ˈeɪdɒlf ˈhɪtlə/ (n.): leader of the Nazis who came to power in Germany in 1933 and persecuted Jewish people came to power in Germany as the leader of the NazisNazis /ˈnɑːtsiz/ (n.): members of Adolf Hitler's political party that ruled Germany and persecuted Jews. He blamed the Jews for all the country's problems. It became impossible for Jews to live normally in Germany. So the Frank family moved to Amsterdam, the Netherlands, where Otto Frank started a new business.

1934: Anne adaptedadapted /əˈdæptɪd/ (v.): adjusted to a new situation or environment; changed to fit in to her new life quickly. She learnt Dutch, made many friends and enjoyed school.

1939: Hitler's armies invadedinvaded /ɪnˈveɪdɪd/ (v.): entered a country by force with an army to take control Poland.

1940: On 10 May, the Germans occupied the Netherlands. The Franks were not safe anymore. The Nazis began imposing restrictiverestrictive /rɪˈstrɪktɪv/ (adj.): limiting freedom; not allowing people to do certain things measures against the Jews. They must wear a yellow star that says "Jew". The Nazis couldn't own a business, take a tram, bus or car; buy sugar or walk on the same street as other people.

1941: Anne had to change schools because a new law said Jewish children could not go to school with other children.

1942: Anne's parents gave her a diarydiary /ˈdaɪəri/ (n.): a personal book in which someone writes their daily experiences and feelings as a present for her 13th birthday on 12 June. The family went into hiding in "The Secret AnnexSecret Annex /ˈsiːkrɪt ˈæneks/ (n.): the hidden rooms in Mr. Frank's office building where the family hid from the Nazis" — a part of Mr. Frank's office. Another family, the Van Pels, joined them on 13 July.

1944: On 4 August, a police van stopped at 263 Prinsengracht. Three Dutch policemen and their Austrian Nazi commander arrested them all. Anne's diary was left scattered on the floor. One of the family's helpers picked it up and kept it.

🔤 What is Alphabetical Order?

Words in the English dictionary are arranged from A (first) to Z (last). This is called alphabetical order. If words start with the same letter, we look at the second letter, then the third, and so on.
